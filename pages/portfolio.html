Gardenhood

Gardenhood is a Ruby on Rails application utilizing Foundation 6, PostgreSQL, Devise, and Amazon Web Services.


Gardenhood allows users to borrow and use each others tools. Instead of having to go buy a new lawn mower for your 10x10 foot patch of grass, you might be able to borrow the nice couple's down the block. And when their power drill breaks, you can pay them in kind by letting them borrow your tool. The idea is simple, but the solution is game-changing.

gitFit is the first application that I have worked on in a collaborative setting. Entering into the project, I was worried about communication, how best to divide and delegate tasks, how to effectively use git and GitHub as a team, and all of the typical fears that come from a history of poor group project experiences in grade school. Old MacDonald's Farm

Initial project for General Assembly, completed two weeks into the program, the assignment was to build a game. I have 6 nephews and nieces all under the age of 5. Some are learning their animals, some are learning colors, and the oldest is obsessed with Simon. They were the inspiration for this version of Simon.


Similar to any other Simon memory game, the player must repeat the randomly generated pattern of colors and sounds. If the player does not repeat the pattern correctly, the game will reset and the player will be prompted to start over. Unless the player chooses to reset the game by clicking on the reset button, the high score will be maintained and displayed.

AUGUST 10, 2016

I'm an educator trying to become a web developer. A school counselor used to relying on soft skills now utilizing newly learned hard skills. And yet, during the first project for our class, I found out just how valuable those soft skills can be in this new endeavor. Relying on persistince, creativity, time management, goal- setting, problem-solving, and communication skills ended up being just as necessary as the knowledge needed to code. In the end, Project 1 left me feeling confident that I could create an MVP, confident that I have the soft skills needed to persist and succeed in this field, and very much aware that I still have so much to learn.

What went well:


  • I had clear a vision from the beginning of what I wanted my game to be, its theme, and its layout and was able to execute that vision
  • The end product met the requirements of the project while also going above and beyond, e.g. implementing a creative theme, creating high score tracking and reset functionality.
  • I understood when to ask for help and was able to form clear and direct questions when needed

Lessons learned and things to be improved upon:


  • I did not have a full grasp on array data structures and the limitations in comparing arrays to arrays. Because I set the game up to compare a computer generated array versus a player generated array, the game does not work exactly as a simon-style game should.
  • I struggled with my game logic attempting to use an if/else statement. Dr. Mike though helped me to see the benefits to using a switch statement instead.
  • In future projects, I want to do a better job of creating and implementing responsive design.
